# Tschumi Principles — Source Grounding for the Reasoning Layer

The rationale layer (see `rationale-schema.md`) must cite at least one principle from this file. This is what separates design reasoning from combinatorial fill.

Each principle below has three parts:
1. **Source** — where Tschumi articulates it
2. **Paraphrase** — the idea in one to three sentences
3. **Operational gloss** — how it maps to the generator's grammar. This is the bridge between text and JSON.

Paraphrases are drawn from Tschumi's published writing on Parc de la Villette and accompanying theoretical work. Direct quotations should be verified against the source before use in published materials; for internal design reasoning, paraphrases are sufficient.

---

## P1 — Disjunction

**Source:** *Architecture and Disjunction* (MIT Press, 1994), essays "Disjunctions" and "Madness and the Combinative"; *Cinegram Folie: Le Parc de la Villette* (Princeton Architectural Press, 1987).

**Paraphrase:** Form, function, and meaning do not coincide and should not be forced into synthesis. Tschumi rejects the functionalist doctrine that program determines form. The folies demonstrate this by being formally identical in origin (the cube) and programmatically distinct (café, kiosk, belvedere, first-aid, daycare).

**Operational gloss:** A folie's form is authored independently of its assigned program. The rationale may name a program, but the program must not explain or justify the geometry. If "this folie is a café, therefore it has a canopy" appears in the rationale, the principle has been violated. Disjunction is cited when the form deliberately resists reading its program.

## P2 — Point / Line / Surface superposition

**Source:** *Cinegram Folie*, diagrams of the Villette overlay system; *Event-Cities* vol. 1 (MIT Press, 1994).

**Paraphrase:** Villette is generated by three autonomous systems laid over one another without hierarchy: **points** (the folies on the 120 m grid), **lines** (the covered walkways and the cinematic promenade), and **surfaces** (the themed gardens and open fields). No system dominates; meaning emerges from their collision.

**Operational gloss:** A folie is a *point*. Its relation to adjacent lines (promenade routes, sight lines between neighbouring folies) and surfaces (the ground plane, any extended horizontal plane it cantilevers over) is a design concern. Cite this principle when the rationale addresses how the folie reads at the grid scale — orientation relative to park axes, relation to the folie at the next grid node, legibility from the promenade.

## P3 — Red as marker of the system

**Source:** *Cinegram Folie*; Tschumi's notes on the competition entry.

**Paraphrase:** The folies are painted red (enamelled steel, approximately RAL 3020) not for expressive reasons but to make the system visible as a system. Red signals that the folie is not a building in the conventional sense — it is a marker, an index of the 120 m grid that governs the park. *Folie* in French means both "pavilion" and "madness"; the colour is the trace of that double meaning.

**Operational gloss:** Never emit non-red primary material. Red is not a choice. Cite this principle only when the rationale foregrounds the folie's role as a grid marker (e.g. "the tower amplifies the point by rising above the tree line and restating the red at sky level").

## P4 — The cube as type / invariant

**Source:** *Cinegram Folie*, "The 10.8 m cube"; *Architecture and Disjunction*, "Madness and the Combinative."

**Paraphrase:** Every folie begins as the same 10.8 m cubic cage. Variation happens *to* this type, never in place of it. The invariance is what allows 26 distinct folies to read as a series rather than 26 individual buildings. Type is the ground; operations are the figure.

**Operational gloss:** Rule 1 of the grammar (frame invariant) and Rule 7 (legibility of the type) enforce this. Cite P4 when the rationale discusses which parts of the cube are preserved to maintain legibility — e.g. "three corners of the upper ring are kept intact so the cube reads through the heavy subtraction on the south face."

## P5 — The 3×3×3 decomposition

**Source:** *Cinegram Folie*, folie generation diagrams; secondary: Louis-Philippe Roux, *Tschumi at Villette* (various analyses).

**Paraphrase:** The cube is not a single volume but a lattice of 27 sub-cubes of 3.6 m each. This internal rhythm is the substrate for every operation: solid cells occupy sub-cube positions, attachments anchor to sub-grid nodes, dislocations shift whole sub-cubes off the lattice. The skeleton is present even when no cell is solid.

**Operational gloss:** Rule 1 enforces the 3×3×3. Cite P5 when the rationale uses the sub-grid as an explicit compositional tool — "two adjacent cells on the bottom tier become a slab that reads as a bench," "the central column is left empty to let the cube be traversable at ground level."

---

## P6 — Program after form

**Source:** *Architecture and Disjunction*, "Disjunctions"; *Cinegram Folie*.

**Paraphrase:** The programs (café, belvedere, information, first-aid, daycare) were assigned to folies after the formal series was designed. A programless folie is a valid folie. Program is not a brief; it is a post-hoc tenancy.

**Operational gloss:** The rationale may name a `program` field, but the form must not be derivable from it. A belvedere folie and an information folie with the same form are both legitimate. Cite P6 when the folie is programless, or when the form deliberately ignores or contradicts its nominal program.

---

## P7 — Cinematic promenade

**Source:** *The Manhattan Transcripts* (Academy Editions, 1981; reissued 1994); *Cinegram Folie*, "Promenade cinématique."

**Paraphrase:** Architecture is experienced in sequence, like cinema — through movement, framing, and discontinuity rather than as a static object. At Villette the covered walkway stitches the folies into a montage: each folie is a frame, the path between them is the cut.

**Operational gloss:** Relevant when the folie is designed to be *approached along a line*. Ramps, stairs, and openings can be oriented to register an arrival direction. Cite P7 when the rationale specifies how the folie reads from a moving vantage — "the wedge on the west face is offset so the form appears open from the promenade and closed from the next folie to the south."

---

## P8 — Repetition produces difference

**Source:** *Architecture and Disjunction*, "Six Concepts" (esp. the concept of *repetition*); *Cinegram Folie*.

**Paraphrase:** The 26 folies are not 26 designs. They are 26 transformations of one design. Repetition is the operation; difference is its product. This is the operative sense in which the series is generative rather than collected.

**Operational gloss:** Use the `relation_to_predecessors` field in the rationale. State explicitly how this folie varies from prior folies in the set — which operation is different, which cells move, which attachment is newly introduced. Cite P8 whenever the rationale foregrounds variation as a design move (not as an accident of seed).

---

## How to cite in a rationale

The `principles_cited` field takes one or more of: `P1` through `P8`, or the short label (`disjunction`, `point-line-surface`, `red-as-marker`, `cube-as-type`, `3x3x3`, `program-after-form`, `cinematic-promenade`, `repetition-difference`).

A good rationale cites one to three principles. Citing all eight is a signal of compliance theatre — the reasoning has not actually committed to a move.

## What is NOT in this file

- Biographical notes on Tschumi
- General architectural theory not specific to Villette
- Principles drawn from later Tschumi projects (Le Fresnoy, Alésia) unless they restate a Villette principle
- Secondary interpretations that have not been corroborated by Tschumi's own writing

If a useful principle emerges during use that is not here, propose it as a PR with a source citation. The reasoning layer works only if its vocabulary is closed and verifiable.
